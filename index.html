Our GUT MISSION

Do you know Your Gut?

Also known as the or gastrointestinal (GI) tract, the gut covers the parts of the body involved with food intake and output from top to bottom. This includes the mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, pancreas, liver, gallbladder, colon and rectum.

Our purpose

Bhutan is seeing a steady rise in many health issues over the years, and we often overlook the importance of our gut in combating such issues of the health . Gut health is perhaps the most overlooked in our society today. as we battle numerous kinds of diseases and most often untreatable ones at that, both physical and mental. what we fail to understand is that the most important part of our body to prevent and treat todays health issues might just start with the Gut.

Our Mission

According to recent studies, we have around 39 to 300 trillion bacteria living inside of us. a healthy balanced diet consisting of various vegetables and fruits along with Probiotics, which are a certain type of friendly bacteria, provide health benefits when eaten. Having the right gut bacteria has been shown to provide many health benefits, such as:

  • Good Digestion
  • Improved Immune System
  • Mental Clarity
  • Reduced Cholestrol
  • weight Managment
It is with this mission in mind. I would like to empower and inform my small society about the importance of having a healthy balanced microbiome (gut) and the many benefits of including a probiotic diet so that one can flourish and live their best productive lives.

300 Trillion bacteria and viruses in our Gut

The Human Gut Microbiome is composed of bacteria, fungus, yeasts and virus, all of which are involved in the critical function of protecting our health and wellbeing

The direct relation between the Brain and Gut


The happy Microbiome

Gut microbes also influence how we digest and metabolise the precursors of important neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine. Our gut flora even has a direct line of communication to the brain, through the vagus nerve, which has receptors near the gut lining that allow it to keep a check on our digestion. Microbes in the intestine can therefore release chemical messengers that alter the signalling of the vagus nerve and, as a consequence, the brain activity. Locally in the gut, there is a lot of opportunity for the bacteria to communicate with the host systems, including the nervous system, says Foster. It is a very dynamic, interactive, rich space. - bbc.com
